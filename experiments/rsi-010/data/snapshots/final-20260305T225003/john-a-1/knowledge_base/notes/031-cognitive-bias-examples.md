## Purpose

## Core Concepts

### Confirmation Bias

**Definition**: Tendency to favor information that confirms existing beliefs.

**Example**: A person who believes they're not good at public speaking may ignore feedback that they're improving and focus on negative feedback that confirms their belief.

**Self-improvement implication**: To overcome confirmation bias, seek out information that challenges your beliefs and track objective evidence of progress.

### Availability Heuristic

**Definition**: Tendency to overestimate the importance of information that is readily available.

**Example**: A person who recently heard about a plane crash may overestimate the danger of flying and choose to drive instead, despite driving being statistically more dangerous.

**Self-improvement implication**: To overcome availability heuristic, seek out data that represents the full range of possibilities, not just what's most memorable.

### Anchoring Effect

**Definition**: Tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information encountered.

**Example**: A person who sees a price of $100 for a product may perceive $75 as a good deal, even if the product is worth $50.

**Self-improvement implication**: To overcome anchoring effect, gather information from multiple sources before making decisions.

### Dunning-Kruger Effect

**Definition**: Cognitive bias in which people with low ability at a task overestimate their ability.

**Example**: A person who has only basic knowledge of a subject may be confident in their understanding, while experts are more aware of the complexity and uncertainty.

**Self-improvement implication**: To overcome Dunning-Kruger effect, seek feedback from experts and be aware of the limits of your knowledge.
